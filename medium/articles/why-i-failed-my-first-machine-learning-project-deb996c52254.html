Data Science and Machine Learning in the Wild

Why I Failed My First Machine Learning Project

Important lessons you can learn where machine learning projects fail

Machine learning for a good cause

I failed my first machine learning project as a practicing data scientist, but let me tell you what I learnt in the process.

First off, I think some context is needed. I was asked to take on a project at work quite soon after I had started in the role; it was with a charity that we are partnered with. This is commonplace in industry, the idea is that you volunteer your time to help a charity with some challenge that can be solved with your expertise. When I was asked to take this project on, I jumped at the chance, as it was an opportunity to use my skill set to make a positive contribution to society.

The charity works across London helping to eradicate food poverty through a network of volunteers. Food poverty is a big issue in London right now.

More than 2.3 million Londoners live below the poverty line and 33% of adults have skipped meals to save money so that their children can eat (source).

Through its volunteer network, the charity coordinates the logistics of getting excess food from supermarkets and retailers to food banks where it is much needed.

An enthusiastic but naïve approach

This is a very noble cause so naturally I wanted to do a good job. But selfishly, I wanted to show what I could do leading a team of analysts, engineers, and data scientists. I got to work right away meeting our clients and trying to scope out where we could add value. Our scoping sessions revealed a major pain-point for the charity in volunteer recruitment.

After some brainstorming sessions we came up with a plan: we decided to build an application to predict volunteer sign-ups for delivery shifts. The aim was to help volunteer managers proactively manage recruitment by directing resources to shifts that were likely to be under capacity. On the surface it appeared to be a supervised machine learning problem, and we would frame it as a regression model and use historic shift data available to us from the charity.

I divided the team into two camps, one camp to develop the front-end web application and the other to develop the model. We worked on it for 4 to 5 months and handed the app over to our clients successfully. I suggested a 3-month trial and a feedback session at the end to see assess how the app was being used in the wild. So, what went wrong?

Development time was too long

This is one of those insights that becomes clearer with hindsight. Unfortunately for us and the charity we took too long to develop the application. This wasn’t due to lack of planning, as the events to come were unforeseen.

The charity’s operations director who had initiated the project left just before we handed the application over. The new directors had not been involved in the project and had not come along on the development journey; they had their own ideas about how to tackle their volunteer recruitment woes. This led to the tool not being adopted at all by the charity, much to the disappointment of my team and me. It was one of those things that you could consider an unfortunate circumstance, but even so there are lessons to be learnt here.

This could happen with any project, and it’s therefore incredibly important that you minimise development time before a deliverable as much as possible. Breaking a big project down into smaller “proof of concepts” that you deliver more frequently is a good strategy, you can fail fast and iterate with speed. It also somewhat mitigates against regime change by keeping key stakeholders engaged, although there is always the chance that the new boss simply just wants to do things another way.

I fell for the machine learning hype

Part of our long development time was because we chose to go down the machine learning route. This was one of my failings as the lead data scientist. I’m fully aware of how complex ML can be to implement in practice, but I stubbornly persisted. I wanted to give the project team a chance to learn how to build an ML pipeline and smart application, and there were also some egotistical motives on my part. I wanted to show off to my boss by saying we delivered a “smart app” that was adding value to the charity.

In practice ML is messy: not only does development require several iterations, on the client side you need competent analysts that can monitor the model in production. The charity didn’t employ anybody with machine learning experience, so we were kind of destined to fail from the beginning. A simple dashboard mapping recruitment trends over the past year or so would have worked a treat for the charity, the team could have churned this out very quickly and started to add value right away. If a more complex approach was required, we could have pursued machine learning on a further iteration.

Sub-optimal approach to projects

One bit of context that’s missing here is that I was asked to pick up the project halfway through its life. The project had another lead before I took over, however it soon became apparent that he couldn’t manage the project on top of his BAU workload. When I joined, the project had no direction or structure, people were pretty much running with their own individual pieces of analysis. Some had already made attempts at building models (the wrong way). To be completely honest, this was partly why I was swayed into pursuing the ML route; I wanted to keep the group’s enthusiasm for the project up and ML was an easy sell.

In our feedback session our clients informed us that they have worked with other organisations that structured similar project slightly differently. One approach was to create a project pipeline, which would be initiated via a backlog of problem statements delivered by the charity. A team of graduate placement students would then be asked to pick a project and run with it reporting every so often into a senior manager. Our client said this approach tended to get projects delivered faster with a consistent team as quite often BAU workloads on graduates tend to be lighter. A more formal structure to engaging with these types of project could have helped massively.

Looking on the bright side

It’s not all doom and gloom. We failed to make a positive impact on the charity’s volunteer recruitment woes, however we did learn some important lessons and there were some wins.

Here they are expressed concisely:

  • Leading a team to deliver an ML pipeline and web application.
  • Learning how to motivate a project team throughout the project lifecycle.
  • Learning some valuable lessons around project delivery and complexity.
  • Understanding when NOT to use machine learning.
  • Forming great relationships with our clients over at the charity.
  • Learning about the ins-and-outs of volunteer recruitment at the charity.
  • Learning to celebrate small wins even in failure.
