The Last-Mile Problems of AI

Three Types of Problems and Five Tactical Solutions

TLDR: Last-mile problems are the final hurdles to realizing AI’s promised values. Reaping the benefits of AI systems requires more than solid business cases, well-executed AI implementations, and powerful technology stacks. It often requires the collaboration of AI and people to provide the right experience to customers. But, companies usually struggle to do this well.

Many analysis highlights how to build AI systems from the lenses of executives and data scientists. Instead, this case study looks at the issues using a personal anecdote and from new perspectives: the ones through the eyes of front line staff and customers. I discuss various practical solutions, such as 80–20 rules in AI and smoothing hand-offs between machines and humans, to help teams to overcome the last mile hurdles of AI delivery in the real world.

To start, let me share a story…

Jess, my wife, and I went shopping at Eaton Center in downtown Toronto recently. Jess was in a very good mood because she just came back from a Hackathon (a 3-day ideation and prototyping competition) her company organized. Jess is a Financial Advisor at a bank in Toronto. She was describing how fantastic (and unreal) all the Artificial Intelligence (AI) ideas and prototypes were as we stopped at a vendor booth to update my phone plan.

A lady, named Joanne and probably in her early 20s, welcomed us and suggested a few good options; I signed up for one of her suggestions. While Joanne was processing the transaction, she turned to Jess and said: “Looks like there is a special offer for you, would you like to hear about it?”.

“Of course!” Jess said.

Joanne looked at the screen for 2 seconds and said: “We’d like to offer you 10G of data for $75 per month. It’s an amazing deal!”

Jess looked puzzled and said: “That doesn’t make sense. I am only paying $65 for the same amount of data and service.”

All of us laughed to defuse the awkwardness. And Joanne went back to complete my transaction. We left in 5 minutes.

On our way home, Jess mentioned how disappointing the AI was, and joked how big companies could not get it right and miss a sale opportunity. She said the AI prototypes she saw at the hackathon is much more capable.

I said: “Well, that’s the difference between AI in the real world and the lab”. She looked impressed at what I said, finally.

Let’s align on the background and the definition of AI systems before breaking our experience down to highlight a few common issues of AI systems. Feel free to skip this if you are familiar with the concept.

How was Jess’s offer generated? In this story, the offer Joanne presented was generated by a Recommender System (RS). RS helps drive sales by analyzing customer behaviours and offering personalized products and services. Some RS rely on a machine learning algorithm called Collaborative Filtering to decide what the best offers are for customers; some RS rely on simpler algorithms, such as logistic regression or rule-based methods, while some use more advanced algorithms, like Deep Reinforcement Learning. Amazon and many e-Commerce and digital companies were early adopters of RS. Many traditional businesses (e.g. Telco and banking) followed shortly after and are in the journey of defining their own ways.

What is an AI system? RS is an example of an AI system. More specifically, AI systems are full-stack solutions (e.g. front and back ends) that offer a context-aware experience to users by processing a large amount of data using Big Data technology and choosing appropriate interactions based on autonomous machine learning algorithms.

There are a few distinctive differences between an AI system and traditional software solutions. Here is the most important one: AI systems interact with users based on autonomous and continuous learning and decisioning, instead of following a set of static and pre-designed rules by designers and engineers.

Let’s connect the dots. In the story, the RS recognized that Joanne (an end-user) was discussing product changes (context awareness) with Jess and me; it provided an offer (an interaction based on Big Data and machine learning) that Joanne could share. The offer would probably be different given another customer and context (e.g. calling to complain about services, instead of considering product change). The RS is a full-stack solution because it has a front-end interface to interact with Joanne and a back-end to support data storage, machine learning, and system integration.

There are many other examples of AI systems, such as the self-driving car, robo-advisor, Alpha-Go, Siri, robots from Boston Dynamics, Roomba, etc.

Now, what’s wrong? The story highlights a number of common ways an AI system can fail in the real world. Let’s break it down.

  1. Wrong Interaction: asking a customer to pay an extra $10 for the same service simply does not make sense. I do not believe any marketing and customer behaviour theory will support this offer.
  2. Poor Execution: although she’s very well-trained, capable, and personable, Joanne clearly didn’t understand why the offer was suggested by the AI system, and therefore, couldn’t properly execute the offer (even when it mean not executing).
  3. Lack of Feedback Loop: after we all realized the offer didn’t make sense, Joanne simply completed my transaction and didn’t provide any feedback to the AI system to help it learn. Quality feedback is critical to the long term success of any AI system.

This example highlights a fundamental and common issue: the disconnect between humans and AI systems. Human-AI collaboration is the last mile problem of AI systems.

UX designers may argue that this is just a typical solution that was poorly designed. Applying Design Thinking and best practices of interface design should solve the problem. Correct, but only to some degree. The unique feature of autonomous interaction requires us to think deeper. If we cannot fully control and comprehend the responses from AI systems, how do we pre-design the key, if not all, aspects of the user interaction?

So, what are the solutions? Here I propose a number of tactical solutions to help companies to maximize the promises of AI systems.

1. From Wrong to Right (or Better) Interactions

Asking the AI systems to provide the right answers 100% of the time is impossible, both mathematically and practically. So, the best we should aim for is having the right enough answer with a reasonable amount of investment. To do this, I suggest exploring the following,

  • Identify an 80–20 AI rescue plan. 80–20 is a well-known concept and business axiom; it highlights the general observation that 20% of the work leads to 80% of the results, roughly. Using the 80–20 mindset to simplify the solution is critical; doing so can help avoid launching a faulty AI system and compromising customer experience (Jess’s example). Clearly, something technical is broken when the AI systems are providing non-sensible recommendations. Overly complicated design is a common issue. A team of data scientists can ask: 1) what are the simplest design to achieve, for example, 80% accuracy? 2) Is the result good enough to deliver the promised business values? and 3) What are the more advanced techniques that can capture the next 2, 5, and 10%? Despite the hype around sophisticated AI techniques, such as Deep and Reinforcement Learning, not everyone needs to use them on day 1. Simple data mining techniques, such as Association Rule, may be able to capture most of the customer dynamics and offer good enough results in the early days.
  • Apply business rules to safeguard. Since machine learning models are bound to have errors, having an extra layer of business rules is imperative for quality assurance. For example, simple rules based on business domain knowledge can look like this: 1) not to offer a more expensive package without extra meaningful services and 2) suppress overly discounted offer that may lead to revenue loss. It is important to avoid complicated rules. They may defeat the purpose of the AI system and ultimately comprise the performance. To identify these rules, working with experienced business managers and front line staff are the keys. This leads to the next suggestion.
  • Sanity check using the right experts. AI systems are often envisioned by business executives and data scientists. Having top-level support with enough technical horsepower is vital to launching successful AI systems. However, when it comes to using the systems that aim to provide better customer interactions, the front line experts know the best and have the most insightful observations. So, it is important to include front line staff in the process despite they may not be the technical experts.

2. From Poor to Better Execution

Most AI systems rely on human staffs to act at some point, good execution is the key to realizing the promised business value. Good execution comes down to this one component: smooth hand-offs between machines and humans. To achieve smooth hand-offs, both AI systems and humans need to work together…

  • Provide instructions that focus on the whats, whys, and hows. Data scientists spend lots of time fine-tuning algorithms, but often neglect to provide the necessary information to help human staffs to be successful. The key is to enable AI systems to explain why the certain recommendation is provided and share simple instructions for the human staffs to follow. A simple what-why-how template can do the trick, see an example below; the idea was originally proposed for leadership motivational speech by Simon Sinek.
  • Up-skill staff to collaborate with AI systems. Being able to use technology is an essential part of many onboarding training. But it is not enough today. AI systems are different than traditional Customer Relationship Management (CRM) applications, for example. Based on their learning and insights, AI systems may provide recommendations that are not obvious to staff. That said, human staffs need to engage at a higher cognitive level to understand, digest, and challenge if needed; staff cannot, and should not, follow instructions blindly. Staffs should treat the AI systems as a partner, who they should collaborate with, challenge, and cultivate.

Imagine this: the RS provides three simple lines that Joanne can digest and follow in less than 10 seconds (or even shorter)…

  • what to do: propose a 10GB data plan upgrade and new phone cash-back for $70 per month
  • why you should do it: the customer (Jess) has been using our services for over 10 years; based on similar customers of her profile, she’s likely to be interested and she responded well to monthly saving
  • how you can approach: tell Jess you appreciate her business for over 10 years, you’d like to offer a good deal that other loyal customers appreciated; it’s a 25% saving per month (or this can be a pre-populated script but with the tailored modification based on Jess’s profile)

Working with AI systems is more complicated, but the rewards — material business benefits and more engaged and valued staff — can be worth it.

3. From Troublesome to Engaging Feedback Mechanism

Lastly, the true power of AI systems comes from their capabilities to learn and improve. However, they cannot do it without meaningful feedback from the environment, front line staff in this case. Meaningful feedback refers to information that describes the whys of customer behaviours. Many front line staff ignore the opportunity to capture and share this information due to clumsy UI and a lack of incentive. Here are a few simple considerations…

  • Develop an easy- and fun-to-use feedback UI. There are two key considerations. First, the UI needs to be simple so the staff can input information easily and accurately so they will not resist doing so. The most important information to capture is why the customer did not respond to the recommendation. Second, gamification, such as using badges and healthy peer competition, can help to encourage and nudge staff to provide quality feedback without hard reinforcement. This is where traditional tricks in CRM management still work.
  • Provide an incentive to input quality feedback. Realistically, it is hard to expect staff to go the extra mile since many are already overwhelmed by the business-as-usual tasks and targets. Therefore, having concrete incentives is the best solution to drive behaviour change. For instance, teams can set up leaderboard and prize for “AI trainer of the month” or formalized a goal in the annual performance review.

Once the feedback is captured, data science teams need to tie it with the learning mechanism of the AI systems. It is tricky from a technical standpoint, there are lots of considerations around the timing of processing. Yet, once implemented, AI systems can learn from the unique information about customers, fix flaws in the systems’ learning, and improve continuously and autonomously. More meaningful feedback leads to smarter AI systems that can provide exponential business benefits.

By exploring and implementing these considerations, the benefit is to increase the probability of success of the AI system, especially when they interact with humans in the real world.

Let’s wrap up…

The RS example only revealed some, but representative, issues of AI systems. As the investment of AI translate into real-world solutions — many will interact closely with front line staff and customers — companies should pause and sanity checks their thinking on the human-AI collaboration experience. Here is where disconnections usually happen. Also, keep in mind that many best practices in technology development, such as “start small and iterate” and “being user-centric”, still apply.

Overcoming the last mile problems is crucial in realizing the heart-warming promises of AI. Having a holistic diagnosis and surgical fixes is critical. Early and lightweight solutions that improve the interactions, executions, and feedback mechanics can help companies to avoid major setbacks in deploying AI systems in the real world.

Fruit for Thoughts

  • what are some unique operational challenges for your team in using AI systems (e.g. time, skills, and/or trust)?
  • how can AI systems help your business to cut costs by streamlining processes (especially the ones that were hard to automate before)?
  • how can AI systems allow your business to create new ways to offer products and engage customers?
