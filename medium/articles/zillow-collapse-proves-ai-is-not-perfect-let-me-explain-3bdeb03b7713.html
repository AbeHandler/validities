Zillow Collapse Proves AI is not Perfect: Let Me Explain

2 Lessons that I learned from the story of Zillow collapse

The recent pandemic causes many things around us to get changed including the real-estate market. The drastic changes in the real estate market cause the machine learning models used to predict house prices don’t work anymore. That is why Zillow collapsed mostly since they rely too much on their AI algorithm to predict house prices.

The house-flipping division at Zillow gets entirely closed after they admit their house price prediction algorithm, also called Zestimate, could not create value for their business anymore. After the pandemic, the algorithm could not forecast housing prices with sufficient accuracy that can make them decide better to buy and sell properties. The algorithm still provides guestimates on housing prices for their online users. The question is “Knowing that failure, will you still use these results?”

The house price prediction is one of the classic examples of data science applications next to “predicting the survival of Titanic passengers” or “identifying handwritten digits on MNIST dataset”. The Zillow collapse can not take house price prediction out of the list of classic machine learning challenges; however, it reminds us where an AI solution can fail and work. In this article, I would like to share two lessons that are worth taking from this incident.

— AI fails when history doesn't rhyme.

History does not repeat itself but often rhymes. When it rhymes, it can be predicted by AI algorithms. In this context, the prediction refers to the future price of a house after renovation within a range of a few months. The volatility that we experienced in the labor market or supply chain during the past two years, makes the prediction near impossible compared to the times before the pandemic.

AI is a powerful tool for prediction; however, it only works if the future is built upon the past. Things that happen after the pandemic did not occur before; so AI algorithms that rely on historical data may not work well. Whenever an element of time kicks in the problem we must be more careful. That is why estimation is an easier task compared to prediction. Here, estimation refers to the current price of a house given a list of features such as location, size, and year of building.

— Ensemble, first, Neural Networks, later.

Ensemble methods are useful in the industry where you need fast results with constant improvements such as early days of development. When you show how AI can create value for the business, then more complex methods with higher performance such as neural networks must be used. I have heard failure stories that executives ask to develop neural network models from the early days while the value of an AI model is not proved yet. Insisting to build a neural network model during the early days increases the chance of failure for your team. Why? Mostly because it is hard to train them properly or the value of the AI engine (regardless of the implementation technique) is not yet understood within the company. Zestimate, the Zillow algorithm to predict house price, began as an ensemble of roughly 1,000 models. You don’t wonder now! After they were able to create value with their AI algorithm, they switch to the neural networks model. The new approach reduced the algorithm errors by about 10% while it creates an opportunity to update the model more frequently. Zillow’s data science team even had started to explore other AI techniques such as document understanding or natural language processing before they fail due to the pandemic. The last point is when the problem became unsolvable due to a big data drift, it doesn’t matter what technique you use. It is not just solvable!

Insisting to build a neural network model during the early days increases the chance of failure for your team.

The Last Words

In short, the lessons that I learned were: “AI fails when history doesn’t rhyme.” and “Ensemble, first, Neural Networks, later”. I would like to hear what you think about this incident.
